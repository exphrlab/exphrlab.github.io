Ongoing Projects

iSTEP

iSTEP (Inspiring Seniors Toward Exercise Promotion) is a research study exploring how to best support older adults in developing regular exercise habits.

Designed for adults aged 65 and older who are not currently exercising regularly, the study examines how personalized music playlists may enhance motivation, enjoyment, and psychological responses during exercise — and whether music can help make physical activity more sustainable over time.

Participants take part in a 6-month hybrid exercise program. They are randomly assigned to exercise with music or without music, and engage in band-based strength training (such as squats, deadlifts, and bicep curls) to support muscle function and healthy aging. In addition, they complete about 150 minutes of aerobic activity per week, following the national physical activity guidelines for older adults, to promote cardiovascular health and endurance.

The program gradually transitions participants toward independent exercise — starting with supervised sessions three days per week, tapering to once per week, and eventually moving into self-directed workouts.

Our long-term goal is to help older adults build lasting, enjoyable, and safe exercise routines that enhance both health and quality of life.

TRAC

TRAC (Tracking Risk Factors and Activity for Cognitive Health) is a longitudinal research study examining how everyday lifestyle factors influence cognitive health across adulthood and aging. The study focuses on identifying which baseline habits — including diet, physical activity, sleep, and body composition — may contribute to age-related changes in cognition, and how changes in these behaviors over time may shape cognitive decline or resilience. We also investigate the underlying physiological and biomarker mechanisms that help explain how lifestyle factors protect the brain throughout the aging process.

Participants complete cognitive and physical assessments, the ASA24 Dietary Intake Tool, and the InBody Body Composition Analysis every six months. Individuals are invited (but not required) to continue participation for up to ten years, allowing us to track longitudinal changes over time. Although this study does not provide treatment, participants receive personalized results from these assessments, including vascular health measures, body composition data, cognitive scores from the NIH Toolbox (standardized to national norms), and physical performance outcomes.

TRAC is designed to advance scientific understanding of how lifestyle behaviors contribute to cognitive aging — while also providing participants with meaningful feedback to help them stay informed and proactive about their health as they age.
